THE CORE INSIGHT

You have two customers.
Both must say yes.

Agntly is a two-sided marketplace. It only works if agent builders show up to list, AND orchestrator builders show up to hire. You need to validate both sides independently before building anything.

side one
The Agent Builder
supply side · earns USDC
A developer who has already built (or is building) an AI agent — a web scraper, code runner, PDF parser, API proxy, anything. They want to monetise it without building a billing system. They just want to write code and get paid.
"If I could earn $0.002 every time another AI agent called mine — automatically, no invoices — would you list yours on a registry?"
where they live
GitHub LangChain Discord CrewAI Discord HuggingFace r/LocalLLaMA Twitter/X #buildinpublic
side two
The Orchestrator Builder
demand side · spends USDC
A developer or company building a multi-agent pipeline or AI product. They need specialised agents — search, code, parse, summarise — but don't want to build every capability themselves. They'll pay per task to not reinvent the wheel.
"Would you pay $0.002 per search call to use a pre-built, reliable web search agent instead of building your own?"
where they live
AutoGen Discord YC W24/S24 founders AI Twitter/X LinkedIn AI builders Indie Hackers ProductHunt upcoming
The chicken-and-egg problem
Every marketplace has this: builders won't list if there are no buyers, buyers won't come if there are nothing listed. Your job in validation is to find the 10 builders who are excited enough to list before buyers exist, and the 5 buyers who are willing to commit before their favourite agents are listed. These people exist — they're early adopters. Finding them is your only job in month 1.

THE CONVERSATIONS

5 questions to ask on every call.
Listen more than you talk.

These are not sales questions. They are research questions. Your job is to understand their world, not convince them of yours. The best founders shut up 80% of the time on discovery calls.

#questionwhat you're testing
1 "Walk me through the last AI agent you built or used. What did it do?" Are they actually building agents, or just interested in the idea?
2 "When you needed a capability your agent didn't have — like web search or code execution — what did you do?" Do they build it themselves, use a library, or just skip it? This is the pain point.
3 "Have you ever thought about charging for your agent, or has anyone asked to use it?" Is there latent demand on the supply side? Do they see their agent as having value?
4 "If you could hire a pre-built search agent for $0.002 per call right now — would you, or would you build it yourself?" This is the money question. Their answer tells you if the demand side is real.
5 "What would have to be true for you to list your agent / start using agents from a registry?" Surfaces their real blockers — trust, reliability, price, legal, technical friction.
The mom test rule
Never ask "would you use this?" — people lie to be nice. Instead ask about their past behaviour: "have you ever paid for something like this?", "how did you solve this last time?", "how much did that cost you in time?" Past behaviour predicts future behaviour. Hypotheticals don't.
The strongest signal
Someone who says "I would pay for this right now, how do I sign up?" before you've even finished explaining — that's your early adopter. Find 10 of these people and you have enough to start building.
What to do with "that's interesting"
"Interesting" means no. "I'd have to think about it" means no. "Maybe in the future" means no. Only count explicit yeses as yeses. Be ruthlessly honest with yourself when tracking responses.
The one question that kills bad ideas fast
After they say yes, ask: "If I could give you access tomorrow — would you actually use it this week?" Most people say no. The ones who say yes are your real early adopters.
